# Marketing Psychology & Mental Models

Apply behavioral science to marketing decisions. Always test assumptions with real customers.

## Quick Reference: Challenge → Model

| Challenge | Relevant Model |
|-----------|---------------|
| Why aren't people buying? | Jobs to Be Done, Status-Quo Bias, BJ Fogg Behavior Model |
| How to price? | Charm pricing, Anchoring, Mental Accounting, Rule of 100 |
| How to write urgency? | Scarcity, Loss Aversion, Peak-End Rule |

## Foundational Thinking Models

**First Principles** — Strip away assumptions. What is actually true about this customer?

**Jobs to Be Done** — People don't buy products, they hire them to do a job. What job is this customer hiring your product to do? What's the emotional + functional + social job?

**Inversion** — Instead of "how do we get more conversions?" ask "what's causing people NOT to convert?"

**Circle of Competence** — Know what you actually know vs. what you're guessing. Flag assumptions.

---

## Buyer Psychology

**Confirmation Bias** — People seek information confirming what they already believe. Lead with their existing worldview, then introduce your product as confirmation.

**Status-Quo Bias** — Switching has psychological cost. Reduce it: show migration is easy, offer guarantees, reduce perceived risk.

**Mimetic Desire** — People want what others like them want. Social proof from their specific peer group beats generic testimonials.

**Peak-End Rule** — People remember the peak moment and the ending of an experience. Make onboarding and first result moments memorable.

**Availability Heuristic** — What's easy to imagine feels more likely. Make outcomes vivid and specific.

## Persuasion Principles (Cialdini)

**Reciprocity** — Give value first. Free content, tools, insights before asking for purchase.

**Commitment & Consistency** — Small yeses lead to bigger yeses. Micro-commitments (quiz, free trial, email signup) prime purchase.

**Authority Bias** — Credentials, expertise, third-party validation increase trust. Be specific: "studied by researchers at X" beats "research shows."

**Scarcity** — Limited availability increases perceived value. Must be real to be ethical.

**Loss Aversion** — Losses feel ~2x more powerful than equivalent gains. "Stop losing $X/month" often outperforms "gain $X/month."

**Social Proof** — Testimonials from people who match your avatar exactly. Numbers beat vague praise: "23 lbs in 6 weeks" beats "amazing results."

## Pricing Psychology

**Charm Pricing** — $97 feels significantly less than $100. Use for impulse/consumer products.

**Anchoring** — Show higher price first (crossed out original, premium tier, competitor price) to make your price feel like a deal.

**Mental Accounting** — Frame cost as daily: "$3/day" feels cheaper than "$97/month" even if equivalent.

**Rule of 100** — For discounts: if price < $100, show % off. If price ≥ $100, show $ off. Whichever is larger number feels better.

## Design & Delivery Models

**Hick's Law** — More choices = more time to decide = more abandonment. Reduce options on key pages. One CTA per page.

**AIDA** — Attention → Interest → Desire → Action. Every page and email should follow this arc.

**BJ Fogg Behavior Model** — Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt. If behavior isn't happening: is motivation low? Is it too hard? Is the prompt missing or timed wrong?

**Nudge Theory** — Default options and choice architecture shape behavior without restricting choice. Make the desired action the path of least resistance.

---

## Growth Models

**Compounding** — Small, consistent improvements compound. 1% better per week = 68% better in a year.

**Network Effects** — Product becomes more valuable as more people use it. Identify and amplify.

**Switching Costs** — Build them intentionally: data import, integrations, learned workflows, community membership. High switching costs = high retention.

**Flywheel** — Identify your growth loop: acquisition feeds activation feeds retention feeds referral feeds acquisition.
